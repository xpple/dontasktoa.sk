Don't ask to ask!

Every now and then, in online chat rooms I participate in, someone pops up and says something along the lines of:

Any Java experts around?

This is bad form, for several reasons. What the person is actually asking here is:

Any Java experts around who are willing to commit into looking into my problem, whatever that may turn out to be, even if it's not actually related to Java or if someone who doesn't know anything about Java could actually answer my question?

There are plenty of reasons why people who do have the knowledge would not admit to it - let alone answer the question. By asking the question like this, you're asking for more than you think you're asking for.
You're asking people to take responsibility. You're questioning people's confidence in their abilities. You're also unnecessarily walling other people out. I often answer questions related to languages or libraries I have never used, because the answers are (in a programmer's kind of way) trivial.
Alternatively, the question can be seen as:

I have a question about Java but I'm too lazy to actually phrase it in words unless there's someone on the channel who might be able to answer it.

This is just lazy. If you're not willing to do the work to solve your problem, why should we?
So don't ask to ask, just ask. Someone who is idling on the channel and only every now and then glances at what's going on is unlikely to answer to your "asking to ask" question, but your actual problem description may pique their interest and get them to answer.
So, to summarize, don't ask "Any Java experts around?", but rather ask "How do I do [problem] with Java and [other relevant info]?".
